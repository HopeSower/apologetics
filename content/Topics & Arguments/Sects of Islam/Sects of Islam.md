# Sects of Islam: An Overview

Islam is a diverse and multifaceted religion with a rich history that has given rise to many different interpretations and traditions. Over the centuries, differences in theology, jurisprudence, and spirituality have led to the emergence of various sects and movements within Islam. This article provides an overview of the major sects of Islam, listing each by name and linking to a dedicated page for more in-depth information.

---

## Sunni Islam
**Sunni Islam** is the largest branch of Islam, comprising roughly 85–90% of the global Muslim population. Sunni Muslims emphasize the authority of the Quran and the Sunnah (the practices and sayings of Muhammad) as interpreted by the consensus of the early Muslim community. Sunni jurisprudence is further organized into several legal schools (madhabs), including:
- **Hanafi**
- **Maliki**
- **Shafi'i**
- **Hanbali**

For more details, visit the dedicated page: [[Sunni Islam]].

---

## Shia Islam
**Shia Islam** represents a major branch, making up about 10–15% of Muslims worldwide. Shia Muslims hold that leadership of the Muslim community should remain within Muhammad’s family, particularly through his cousin and son-in-law, Ali. Within Shia Islam, several sub-sects have developed, each with its own unique doctrines and practices:

- **Twelver Shia Islam**: The largest Shia group, known for its belief in the Twelve Imams. See [[Twelver Shia Islam]].
- **Ismaili Shia Islam**: Emphasizes the spiritual authority of the Imam and includes several branches such as the Nizari and Musta'li. See [[Ismaili Shia Islam]].
- **Zaidi Shia Islam**: Predominantly found in Yemen, known for its distinctive jurisprudence and views on leadership. See [[Zaidi Shia Islam]].
- **Alevi Islam**: Mainly practiced in Turkey and parts of the Middle East, it incorporates unique rituals and interpretations distinct from mainstream Twelver doctrine. See [[Alevi Islam]].
- **Alawite Islam**: Predominantly found in Syria, this group has evolved its own set of practices and beliefs over time. See [[Alawite Islam]].

---

## Ibadi Islam
**Ibadi Islam** is distinct from both Sunni and Shia traditions. Primarily found in Oman, Zanzibar, and parts of North Africa, Ibadism advocates a puritanical and moderate form of Islam. It emphasizes community consensus and a pragmatic approach to governance and law. For further reading, see [[Ibadi Islam]].

---

## Ahmadiyya Islam
The **Ahmadiyya movement** began in the late 19th century in British India under the leadership of Mirza Ghulam Ahmad, who is regarded by Ahmadis as the promised Messiah and Mahdi. Although many mainstream Muslim groups do not recognize Ahmadis as part of the Islamic fold, the movement has grown significantly worldwide. Explore more on the [[Ahmadiyya Islam]] page.

---

## Quranism
**Quranism** is a movement that regards the Quran as the sole source of religious guidance, rejecting the authority of the hadith literature that underpins much of traditional Islamic jurisprudence and practice. Quranists maintain that the Quran is complete and self-sufficient for guiding a believer’s life. For an in-depth look, see [[Quranism]].

---

## Sufism
**Sufism** represents the mystical dimension of Islam. While not a sect in the strictest sense, Sufism encompasses a wide range of spiritual practices and orders (tariqas) focused on the inner, experiential dimensions of faith. Prominent Sufi orders include:
- **Qadiriyya**
- **Naqshbandiyya**
- **Shadhiliyya**

Sufism has influenced both Sunni and Shia communities and offers a rich tradition of poetry, music, and ritual aimed at fostering a direct experience of the divine. Learn more on the [[Sufism]] page.

---

## Salafism
**Salafism** advocates a return to the practices of the “pious ancestors” (salaf) of early Islam. It emphasizes a literal interpretation of the Quran and Hadith and is often seen as a reformist approach within Sunni Islam. For further details, visit [[Salafism]].

---

## Wahhabism
Closely related to Salafism, **Wahhabism** is a conservative reform movement that originated in 18th-century Arabia. It is closely associated with the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia and calls for a purification of Islamic practices, rejecting what it considers later innovations. Read more on the [[Wahhabism]] page.

---

## Druze
**The Druze** faith originated from Ismaili Shia Islam but has since evolved into a distinct religious tradition with its own beliefs and practices. Although Druze do not identify as Muslims today, their historical roots lie within the Islamic tradition. For a historical overview and insights into their current practices, see [[Druze]].
