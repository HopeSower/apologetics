Sunni Islam is the largest branch of Islam, and its scholarly tradition has developed a rigorous methodology to determine the authenticity of hadiths (the recorded sayings and actions of Muhammad). While the Quran is universally accepted as the direct word of God, hadiths serve as an essential supplementary source of guidance—provided they meet strict criteria for authenticity.

---

## Do All Sunnis Believe in All Hadiths?

Not all hadiths are accepted unconditionally. Sunni scholars have developed a detailed science (*‘ilm al-hadith*) to evaluate each hadith based on its:
- **Chain of Transmission (Isnad):** The reliability and continuity of the chain of narrators.
- **Content (Matn):** Consistency of the hadith’s message with the Quran and established practice.
- **Historical Context:** The circumstances surrounding the narration and its transmission.

Hadiths that pass these tests are categorized as **sahih** (authentic) or **hasan** (good), while those that do not are classified as **da'if** (weak) or **mawdu'** (fabricated). Therefore, only those hadiths that have been rigorously authenticated are accepted.

---

## Authority of the Hadiths

The authority of a hadith in Sunni Islam is determined by:
- **Isnad Evaluation:** Scrutinizing the trustworthiness of each narrator in the chain. This involves detailed analysis of every link in the chain of transmission, as well as a careful examination of the *matn* (the text) of the hadith.
- **Consistency with the Quran:** Ensuring that the hadith does not conflict with the Quran or the established consensus (*ijma*) of the early Muslim community.
- **Scholarly Consensus:** Early scholars such as Imam Bukhari and Imam Muslim, along with later generations, have refined these evaluations to compile the most reliable collections.

## Scriptural Hierarchy in Sunni Islam

- **Primary Scripture:** The **Quran** is the definitive and unaltered word of God.
- **Secondary Guidance:** Authenticated **hadith collections** serve as crucial explanatory texts for understanding and applying the Quranic message.
- **Acceptance:** Hadiths authenticated through classical methods are integrated into legal and theological discussions.
- **Rejection:** Hadiths deemed weak, contradictory, or fabricated are generally excluded or given minimal weight.

---

## Sunni Sub-sects and Hadith Authority

Within Sunni Islam, the major legal schools (madhabs)—**Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali**—share the core collections of hadith, although they may differ slightly in:
- **Methodological Nuances:** Interpretations and applications of hadith in legal rulings.
- **Preference for Certain Narrations:** Variations in emphasis on specific hadiths based on broader interpretative principles.

These differences are relatively minor compared to the shared framework for hadith authentication, so the topic is covered collectively on this page.
